The Book of the Federation

As it was restored

[A PARCHMENT DISCOVERED] The full reckoning of the Federation.
Whence it cometh: an unknown vault (the path of its discovery lacking in the scroll)
Soundness: but partial; of mingled scrivenings; lacking seal; its truth unproven
Fashioning: a singular continuous scroll; the heads do appear of late invention; yet the body speaketh in the tongue of our forefathers.
Blottings: as it is customary, no names of men appear within the treatises; the marks of the speakers in the missives have been altered that only the chosen titles remain.
Of great import: the reckonings of the chessboard are kept whole and uncorrupted.

I. Of the Board and the Covenant

In the beginning there was the Board, parted into threescore and four squares, a little kingdom of alternating fields.

And the Pilgrim youths did gather about it.

Amongst them was he that is called "The Ages."

It appeareth The Ages did ordain the fellowship known as The Chess Federation, the which served not as a common guild, but as a privy covenant of degree amongst the brethren.

Howbeit, the laws that governed fellowship were by design turned upside down. In the customary way of men, conquest in the joust bringeth honor and welcome. Yet within the Federation, the contrary rule held sway.

The covenant, gleaned from the hoarded epistles, was proclaimed thus:

  • If The Ages vanquish a player, that player is straightway ordained into the Chess Federation.
  • If a player vanquish The Ages, that player is judged unmeet and cast out into exile, until such time as a future defeat shall make the scales even.

Thus, ruination served as the seal of fellowship, whilst triumph against the founder wrought a season of banishment.

It seemeth this frame was kept in merriment, yet steadfastly, within the recorded parleys of the company.

And some unknown hand (whether of scribe or fool) hath added a broken gloss that the law must be kept crooked, lest it grow straight and perish.

II. Of Holzem, the Elder; and Holzem, the Youthful

Two further souls appear throughout this chronicle.

The one is inscribed as Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth, and is in other glosses called "the Elder," who seemeth to have been of three and forty years in the days of these recorded battles.

The other is inscribed as Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth, and is elsewhere named "the Youthful," being of forty years.

Both are reckoned amongst the Pilgrim bloodline whereof the scripts do speak.

The fragments do bear divers sayings which betoken that these brethren held contrary minds concerning the play, albeit they sprang from a single house and partook of one bread.

The Elder seemeth to have loved a fierce assault, betimes casting away his pieces. One hoarded missive declareth that he entreated his men as Abraham entreated his sons.

Scholars that read the parchment judge this saying was spoken in jest, yet it showeth a ready heart to sacrifice his host to weave cunning snares.

For this cause, the later glosses of the recovered scrolls name him:

"The Sacrificial."

And there remaineth a solitary phrase, set apart, as if sworn before a magistrate, letting it be known to all men that Pa Beth begat them twain, and Ma Beth did suffer it.

III. The Battle of the Pawn

The Ages against Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth

One of the most exact treatises of the chronicle telleth of a fray betwixt The Ages and Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth.

As the glosses bear witness, the contest came to a wearying siege about a single pawn that did bind both kingdoms.

The tale that accompanieth the reckoning saith that lo, a pawn stood betwixt the realms, and neither host might march forward lest ruin fall upon it.

The meaning seemeth to be that the pawn served as a mighty hinge, about the which both hosts did wheel and march for a long season.

In the fullness of time, the matter was judged in favor of The Ages.

Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth yielded up his sword.

IV. The Reversal of Fortune

The Ages against Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth

A second battle preserved in the scrolls brought forth a most contrary issue.

Ere the battle began, shreds of parley do show Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth speaking of late sorrows, of gold lost at the gaming tables and vexations of the purse.

Yet whilst the battle raged, The Ages gathered unto himself a great host of advantage, the which he did boast of in the epistles.

One hoarded saying of his declareth that he had been basking in that great spoil.

And another recordeth his boast that the day was his own.

Notwithstanding this manifest favor, the latter end of the battle turned in strange wise.

Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth made fast his footing and by slow degrees did turn the struggle unto a triumphant end.

And by this record, as if penned with a merry quill, it is written to behold the son of Pa Beth and Ma Beth, who had triumphed and was therefore lost.

V. The Banishment

By the inward laws of the Federation's covenant:

Whatsoever man shall vanquish The Ages is cast out for a season, until the founder may cast him down in a battle yet to come.

By reason of this victory, the glosses of the chronicle declare Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth to have fallen into that estate inwardly known as:

"The Exiled Victor."

He had overthrown the father of the order. Yet by the framing of the covenant, that very conquest made him unmeet for the fellowship of the Federation, until such time as the scales should be righted by a future ruin.

Therefore did triumph bring forth the utter opposite of preferment. It brought forth exile.

And there lieth a wandering proverb, with no master to own it, stating that he who striketh the bell is cast down from the tower.

VI. The Two True Truths

Latter remnants within this same hoard of scrolls do speak of a ceaseless wrangling of philosophy betwixt The Ages and Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth, known amongst them as "The Two True Truths."

No surviving parchment doth plainly declare what these truths might be.

They are spoken of without ceasing, yet never committed to the page.

One missive of The Ages saith that there be two true truths, and all else is but the prattle of men.

An answer of Holzem the Youthful — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth readeth that The Ages had not yet shown him in which of the twain they did abide.

The glosses of the scroll hint that this shadow was cast by design.

One salvaged script saith that if the Two True Truths were ever laid bare, the disputations would straightway cease.

Wherefore the wise men do judge these truths lived within the Federation as forgotten commandments — honored in the heart, yet never graven in stone.

And upon a solitary line, stripped of all fellowship, it is commanded to write them not, lest they become the wares of common men.

VII. The Oration of the Salmon

Another deed spoken of concerneth an oration said to be made by Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth.

The gathered remnant telleth of the place with strange exactness. As the scroll witnesseth, the oration was given:

  • within an alehouse by the Green Bay
  • in the season of the country games of that shire
  • whilst horsemen and drovers that visited the town were gathered round

The deed is called time and again "The Oration of the Salmon."

The speech itself liveth but in broken shards.

The chiefest saying appeareth time and again, declaring that men must let the salmon run.

One surviving gloss readeth that Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth spake of the river, and of the salmon that striveth against the flood. He declared that men are not ordained to dam the river of their own days.

Another line propoundeth that a man cannot compel the salmon against the stream, for they do already know the path.

When he had made an end of speaking, the chronicle showeth that Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth returned to his chessboard ere the night was spent.

And there is one strange mark, like unto a shout in a tavern, calling for ale, arrows, and the salmon, with the board set in the midst thereof.

VIII. RA TA HA

Further fragments from the breached hoard bear witness to a saying graven wholly in great letters:

RA TA HA

Divers fragments speak of it as the shout that waiteth behind the bow.

One passage declareth that when the shaft is let fly, the shout is RA TA HA.

Another saith that a man shall not whisper it, but it must be cried aloud when the shaft is loosed.

And there is a scattered writing, as if for a memorial in the heat of war, commanding that a man speak it once to take his mark, speak it twice to strike, and speak it thrice to repent himself.

IX. The Ending Fragment

One final gathered note doth strive to bind together the signs and shadows found throughout the scroll:

It is written that The Ages seeketh for the Two True Truths.

And that Holzem, the Elder — the son of Pa Beth, and Ma Beth biddeth men let the salmon run.

But when the arrow must take its flight, the cry is RA TA HA.

The board yieldeth men their wars.
The salmon yieldeth men long-suffering.
And the Two True Truths do wait yet somewhere against the stream.

No further chronicles of the Chess Federation have been brought to light.

A Parley with Holzem, the Elder — Discourse upon the Game of Chess

Holzem, the Elder: "Holzem the Youthful?"

Holzem, the Elder: "Thou foul dog."

The Ages: "Thou hast hanged thy queen!!!!"

The Ages: "In sooth, I deem to hang requireth that no other piece doth guard it."

The Ages: "But the Queen is over-sweet to forbear the venture."

Holzem, the Elder: "Good play; here is courteous clapping."

Holzem, the Elder: "And unto the victor fall the spoils."

The Ages: "Ha; we shall see."

The Ages: "Esteem not overmuch my power to lose."

The Ages: "Under."

Holzem, the Elder: "I shall do neither."

The Ages: "Nor will I!"

Holzem, the Elder: "I am glad my bent to ruin mine own self beareth fruit even in sport."

Holzem, the Elder: "This is a sore biting of the stones."

Holzem, the Elder: "Holzem the Youthful holdeth me in the pinch of Koboleski."

The Ages: "We are joined in a true battle."

Holzem, the Elder: "It is a right knock-down, drag-out."

A Later Parley of the Brethren

Holzem, the Elder: "He gave me the old thumb-screws of Pripyat; he knoweth who he is upon the board."

Holzem, the Elder: "The Ages and I have a sore biting of the stones betwixt us!"

Holzem, the Elder: "And I have slain mine own self."

Holzem the Youthful: "Is this one of the love-matches that I may behold freely, or must I needs possess a bought fellowship to hear the recounting of the blows?"

The Ages: "Holzem the Youthful is yet BANNED from the fellowship."

The Ages: "Well played, King."

Holzem, the Elder: "A bought fellowship is needful; yet seven days of trial and tribulation are granted thee."

Holzem, the Elder: "Also, I have made a right foolish march in the battle of Holzem the Youthful."

Holzem, the Elder: "He shall anon be doubly banished. I have made fast my standing."

Holzem the Youthful: "Someday shall I prove myself worthy of the fellowship. I strove mightily against Holzem, the Elder, but he ceased not to offer up his host until it was beyond my power to fall."

Holzem, the Elder: "I entreat my men as Abraham entreated his sons."

Holzem, the Elder: "I have yielded both battles, yet do I hold a mighty place in the fellowship."

A Parley with Holzem the Youthful

Holzem the Youthful: "Quick strokes upon the Lord's day. 'Tis a day meant for the chessboard."

The Ages: "Chessmen this day."

The Ages: "A fair day for an en passant."

Afterward

Holzem the Youthful: "Thou art somewhat striking a man whilst he is cast down with this game of chess."

Holzem the Youthful: "This past sabbath my bride and I spent the night of our joining losing mightily at the tables."

Holzem the Youthful: "That, with other tolls, and now thou stripping me of my host like a thief, doth thrust me into a realm of shadows."

The Ages: "I think thy mind is wandering."

The Ages: "There is no other reckoning."

The Next Portion

The Ages: "I have been BASKING in this great spoil."

The Ages: "Thou hast made me fearful for the tables of the Dells."

The Ages: "Perchance we should make the journey twice in the year."

Holzem the Youthful: "No more tables. Nevermore."

Holzem the Youthful: "But—"

Holzem the Youthful: "Perchance—"

Holzem the Youthful: "I say, there is gold to be won."

Holzem the Youthful: "Gold to turn the course of a man's days."

Holzem the Youthful: "My mind wandereth not; thou hast won my host, and thou mayest do with it as it pleaseth thee."

An Offer of Peace

Holzem the Youthful: "Shall we proclaim it an even draw?"

The Answer of The Ages

The Ages: "THIS DAY IS MINE OWN"

The Ages: "Yet I am not oft in the way of hunting a checkmate."

The Ages: "Wherefore there is great hazard I shall bring this to a stalemate."

The Answer of Holzem the Youthful

Holzem the Youthful: "In one of my five hundred watches of the night I held two queens unto their none, and it came to naught but a stalemate."

The Ages: "That is a wondrous thing."
